ISOGrading — A Different Take

Please Don't Grade Your Card.

Seriously. We mean it.

Card collecting was built on joy — the hunt, the flip, the community, the stories. Grading turned a hobby into a financial instrument. We think that's a problem.

Before you hand your card to a stranger and wait 9 months for a number, we want you to think hard. This hobby is best enjoyed with friends, family, and collectors from around the world — not locked in a plastic slab on a shelf.

But if you're going to grade — do it right. Do it with us.

Still Thinking About It?
Let's Walk Through It Together.
Answer 8 honest questions. We'll tell you what we really think.
QUESTION 01 OF 08
Are you grading this card specifically for monetary or investment value?
Not "it might be worth more" — specifically, is the financial return the primary reason you're considering grading?
QUESTION 02 OF 08
Would you genuinely enjoy the grading journey itself?
Seeing the final grade, the protection the case provides, the process — not just the result. Be honest with yourself.
QUESTION 02 OF 08
Do you know the current market value of your card ungraded?
Not what you hope it's worth — what it's actually selling for raw, right now, on the open market. Recent comps. Real sales.
QUESTION 03 OF 08
Do you know what it sells for graded at an 8, a 9, AND a 10?
All three grades. Not just the PSA 10 moon number — what does an 8 actually sell for? A 9? You need all three numbers before this decision makes sense.
QUESTION 04 OF 08
Is this card a low-numbered parallel? /1, /5, /10 range?
Super short print parallels rarely gain significant value from grading — unless they hit Gem Mint 10. The scarcity is already priced in.
QUESTION 05 OF 08
Is this card genuinely immaculate?
Perfect corners. Perfect centering. Flawless auto (if applicable). Zero print defects. No dents, cuts, bends, surface scratches, dust, or hair. Not "pretty good" — IMMACULATE.
QUESTION 06 OF 08
Graded at a 7 or 8 — is your card worth at least $20 more than ungraded?
After grading fees, shipping, and wait time — does the graded value at a 7 or 8 still put you meaningfully ahead? Use your real comps from earlier.
QUESTION 07 OF 08
Graded at a 9 — is your card worth $30–$50+ more than ungraded?
For a near-perfect card that might not quite hit 10 — does a PSA/BGS/SGC 9 still justify the cost and risk?
QUESTION 08 OF 08
You need a Gem Mint 10. If it grades a 9 and you lose money — can you live with that?
You are now gambling. The only winning scenario is a 10. If the grader sees something you don't, you lose time and money. Are you genuinely okay with that outcome?
💚
DON'T GRADE IT.
AND THAT'S PERFECTLY FINE.

If there's no financial upside and no personal joy in the process — grading is just an expense and a wait. Put that money toward your next pull instead. Trade it, display it, share it with your crew. That's what this hobby is actually about.

BUT DO IT RIGHT.

You've done the work. The math checks out. Your eyes are open. Now make sure the process is as sharp as your decision. Here's the ISOGrading checklist — follow it every time.

THE ISOGRADING PROCESS
01
Document it first. Add to ISOVault with photos, condition notes, and acquisition cost before it leaves your hands.
02
Confirm your comps one more time. Pull fresh sales in the 30 days before you submit — markets move.
03
Pick the right grader for this card. PSA dominates for vintage and modern stars. BGS for ultra-premium moderns. SGC for vintage raw. Know your buyer's preference.
04
Use proper submission packaging. Card savers, not toploaders. Stamp to prevent sliding. Never ship loose.
05
When it returns — log the grade and cost. Update your ISOVault entry immediately so your market value data stays accurate.
06
List it on GrailISO. Buyers here are searching by grade, variation, and year — your graded card finds its buyer faster.
